RELIGIOUS

"Religious" refers to anything related to religion, which is a system of beliefs, practices, and values that usually revolves around the worship of a deity or deities, the understanding of life, the afterlife, and moral guidelines. People who are "religious" typically engage in practices or rituals that align with the beliefs of a specific faith, such as prayer, meditation, attending services, or following a set of ethical or moral codes. In a broader sense, someone who is religious actively observes or follows the customs, teachings, and principles of their religion. Religions can range from major world religions like Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, Judaism, and others, to smaller or indigenous belief systems..

HISTORY

Religion has evolved significantly throughout history. Early human beliefs often revolved around animism, polytheism, and the worship of natural forces. Around 2000 BCE, Judaism emerged as one of the first monotheistic religions. In India, Hinduism developed, with its rich spiritual texts like the Vedas. Buddhism arose in the 5th century BCE, founded by Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha), teaching the path to enlightenment. In China, Confucianism and Taoism shaped social and ethical philosophy. Christianity emerged in the 1st century CE, rooted in the teachings of Jesus Christ, leading to a global following. Islam followed in the 7th century, emphasizing submission to the will of Allah through the Quran. Throughout history, religion influenced social, political, and cultural structures. Today, numerous belief systems coexist, reflecting humanity's diverse search for meaning.

HINDUISM

Vedic Period (c. 1500 BCE – 500 BCE)

Originated with the arrival of the Indo-Aryans in the Indian subcontinent.

3
Classical or Epic & Puranic Period (c. 500 BCE – 1200 CE)

Development of major Hindu philosophies like Vedanta, Samkhya, and Yoga.

4
Medieval to Modern Period (c. 1200 CE – Present)

Further spread of Bhakti and devotional movements (e.g., Ramanuja, Kabir, Mirabai, Chaitanya).

5
Vedic Stories (Ancient Period – c. 1500 BCE – 500 BCE)

Rooted in the Vedas and early scriptures, these stories focus on creation myths, cosmic order, and divine beings.

6
Itihasa & Puranic Stories (Epic & Classical Period – c. 500 BCE – 1200 CE)

Found in the great epics (Itihasas) and Puranas, these stories focus on dharma (righteousness), devotion, and heroism.

7
Bhakti & Folk Tales (Medieval to Modern Period – c. 1200 CE – Present)

Stories from regional traditions, saints, and Bhakti/Yoga movements emphasizing devotion (Bhakti) and personal connection with the divine.

8
Nirguna Brahman (Formless, Absolute Reality)

God as the formless, infinite, and transcendent reality beyond human comprehension. Described in Upanishads and Advaita Vedanta (philosophy of non-duality). Considered the ultimate reality (Brahman)—without attributes, beyond names and forms. Concepts: "Aham Brahmasmi" (I am Brahman) – the soul (Atman) and Brahman are one. The universe is an illusion (Maya), and true realization is seeing beyond it. Example: Adi Shankaracharya’s philosophy emphasizes that Brahman is beyond worship and cannot be limited by forms.

9
Saguna Brahman (Personal God with Forms & Attributes)

God as a personal deity with qualities, form, and emotions, accessible to devotees. Rooted in the Bhakti tradition and Puranas, making devotion easier for common people. Worshipped in different forms as Brahma (Creator), Vishnu (Preserver), Shiva (Destroyer), and Devi (Divine Mother). Concepts: Vaishnavism – Worship of Vishnu/Krishna/Rama as the supreme God. Shaivism – Worship of Shiva as the ultimate divine force. Shaktism – Worship of Devi (Durga, Kali, Lakshmi, Saraswati) as the supreme power. Example: Krishna in the Bhagavad Gita says, "Whenever there is a decline in righteousness, I incarnate to restore Dharma."

Shruti (Heard/Divine Revelation) – The Core Scriptures

Considered the eternal, divine knowledge directly revealed to sages (Rishis). Most authoritative texts in Hinduism, forming the foundation of Hindu philosophy. Books: Vedas (Rig, Yajur, Sama, Atharva) – Contain hymns, rituals, and spiritual knowledge. Upanishads – Philosophical texts exploring the nature of Brahman (ultimate reality) and the soul (Atman). Example: The famous Upanishadic phrase Tat Tvam Asi ("You are That") signifies unity between the individual soul and the divine.

11
Smriti (Remembered) – Epics & Puranas

These texts are considered secondary to Shruti but are highly influential in shaping Hindu beliefs and practices. Passed down through generations and written by sages based on divine wisdom. Texts: Itihasas (Epics): Ramayana – Story of Lord Rama, an ideal king and person. Mahabharata – Story of the Kurukshetra war, including the Bhagavad Gita, where Krishna teaches Arjuna about duty (Dharma). Puranas – Mythological and devotional stories about gods, creation, and moral teachings (e.g., Vishnu Purana, Shiva Purana, Bhagavata Purana). Example: The Bhagavad Gita teaches that fulfilling one’s duty selflessly is the path to liberation.

12
Sutras & Shastras (Hindu Law, Philosophy, and Yoga)

Texts that provide guidance on philosophy, dharma (ethics), rituals, and yoga. Texts: Dharma Shastras (Law Books) – Manusmriti, Yajnavalkya Smriti (guidelines on duties, law, and social order). Philosophical Texts (Darshanas) – Nyaya, Samkhya, Vedanta, Yoga Sutras (explaining different Hindu philosophies). Yoga Sutras by Patanjali – Explains the path to spiritual enlightenment through meditation and self-discipline. Example: The Yoga Sutras describe the eightfold path (Ashtanga Yoga) for self-realization and liberation.

13
Oral Tradition and Gurus

Hindu religious education traditionally relies heavily on the guru-shishya (teacher-student) relationship. Knowledge is passed down through oral transmission. In ancient times, this included: Vedic Teachings: The Vedas, which are the oldest scriptures of Hinduism, were memorized and recited by students in special learning centers called Gurukulas. This was done through intensive oral study, where the teacher (guru) imparted sacred knowledge directly to the student. The Guru's Role: The guru plays a central role in guiding students on spiritual matters, answering questions, and ensuring the transmission of knowledge in both philosophy and rituals.

14
Sacred Texts and Temples

Hindu religious education also occurs through the study of sacred texts and community participation: Scriptural Education: Hindus study important scriptures such as the Bhagavad Gita, Upanishads, Puranas, and Ramayana. These texts are often studied in religious schools or temples under the guidance of priests or scholars. In some cases, study groups or Satsangs are held for communal learning. Temples as Learning Centers: Temples also serve as places of learning where spiritual discourses and teachings take place. Priests conduct ceremonies and recite sacred texts, often explaining their meanings to the community.

ISLAM

  • Aqeedah (Belief & Faith)

    Refers to the core beliefs in Islam, which every Muslim must hold. Based on the Six Articles of Faith (Iman): Belief in One God (Allah) Belief in Angels Belief in Prophets (from Adam to Muhammad) Belief in Holy Books (Quran, Torah, Gospel, Psalms, Scrolls of Abraham) Belief in Day of Judgment & Afterlife Belief in Divine Decree (Qadr) – Predestination by Allah. Example: The Shahada (Islamic declaration of faith) states, “There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is His messenger.”

  • Ibadah &(Worship & Practice)

    Focuses on the religious duties and acts of worship that every Muslim must follow. Based on the Five Pillars of Islam: Shahada (Faith) – Testifying belief in one God and Muhammad as His prophet. Salah (Prayer) – Performing five daily prayers facing the Kaaba. Zakat (Charity) – Giving a portion of wealth to the poor. Sawm (Fasting) – Observing fast during Ramadan. Hajj (Pilgrimage) – Visiting Mecca once in a lifetime if able. Example: Muslims pray five times a day as an act of devotion and obedience to Allah.

  • Akhlaq & (Ethics & Morality)

    Defines the moral and ethical code that governs a Muslim’s behavior in society. Includes honesty, kindness, justice, patience, and respect for others. Emphasized in the Quran and Hadith (sayings of Prophet Muhammad). Example: The Prophet Muhammad said, “The best among you are those who have the best manners.” (Hadith - Bukhari & Muslim)

Christianity

Christianity is based on the belief in one God and the divinity of Jesus Christ, who Christians believe is the Son of God. Jesus' life, death, and resurrection are seen as central to salvation.

Christians follow the teachings of the Bible, which is divided into two parts: the Old Testament (shared with Judaism) and the New Testament, which focuses on the life and teachings of Jesus, along with early Christian writings.

Christianity teaches that salvation, or eternal life with God, is achieved through faith in Jesus Christians believe Jesus' sacrifice on the cross atoned for human sin, offering forgiveness and reconciliation with God.
